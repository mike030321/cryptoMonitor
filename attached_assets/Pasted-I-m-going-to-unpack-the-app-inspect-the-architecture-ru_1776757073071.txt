Bottom line

The app is failing for multiple structural reasons, not one bug.

The biggest problem is this:

your system is optimizing for “being directionally right by a tiny amount,” not for making money after costs.

That mismatch shows up everywhere:

prediction scoring
confidence calibration
model fusion
trade entry thresholds
evaluation metrics
agent evolution

So the bots can look “smart” on dashboard stats and still lose money or trade badly.

Your own included snapshots already show that problem:

one snapshot shows 54.73% accuracy with 277 trades and -0.80% P&L
another shows 58.62% accuracy, but the “edge” is mainly relative to a falling buy-and-hold basket; the bots were not producing a strong absolute profit engine
The most important issues
1) The app scores predictions on moves that are too small to trade profitably

In monitor.ts, the prediction resolution thresholds are:

5m: 0.05%
1h: 0.15%
2h: 0.30%
6h: 0.70%
1d: 1.50%

But in paper-trader.ts, round-trip friction is modeled as:

fee 0.10% per side
slippage 0.05% per side
total round trip = 0.30%

That means:

a 5m prediction can be counted correct on a 0.05% move
but a trade needs to beat ~0.30% costs just to break even

So the learning signal is wrong. The system rewards agents for calling tiny noise moves that are not tradable.

This is the single biggest reason the system can show decent accuracy and still fail in P&L.

2) Confidence calibration is backwards

In confidence-calibrator.ts, overallBias is computed as:

actualWinRate - confidence midpoint

So:

positive bias means the agent was better than stated confidence
negative bias means the agent was worse than stated confidence

But the file labels it the wrong way around:

positive bias is called OVERCONFIDENT
negative bias is called UNDERCONFIDENT

Then it applies calibration like this:

adjustedByBias = rawConfidence - overallBias * 0.15

That is backwards too.

Result:

when an agent is actually underconfident, the system may reduce confidence further
when an agent is actually overconfident, the system may not penalize correctly

That poisons both:

the prompt feedback to the models
the confidence used for trading decisions

This is a real bug, not just a strategy opinion.

3) “Stable / no trade” is mostly broken at the parser layer

Your system conceptually supports "stable", but parseModelResponse() in ai-engine.ts only accepts "up" or "down".

So even if a model wants to abstain or output stable, that response gets rejected.

That means the system is biased toward forced directional calls unless:

both models disagree enough to trigger no-trade logic
or another fallback path suppresses trading

This is bad because your intended edge clearly depends on knowing when not to trade.

4) The app uses synthetic historical price data with random jitter

In monitor.ts, seedHistoricalPriceData() takes daily OHLCV and fabricates intraday prices by interpolating between OHLC levels and adding random jitter.

That means your technical indicators may initially be built on:

fake path structure
fake intraday volatility
fake crossover timing

That contaminates:

RSI
MACD
Bollinger
EMA crossover
ATR
pattern labels
fingerprint matching
contagion logic
historical accuracy context

So the model may be reasoning from synthetic market behavior, especially early on or in sparse-history situations.

5) Agent differentiation is mostly cosmetic

The agents have different personalities, but they all consume mostly the same:

price history
indicators
news
market sentiment
calibration
consensus engine
thresholds
trade logic

So the fleet looks diverse in the UI, but under the hood it is mostly:
same data + same engine + different prompt flavor

That means you do not really have 10–15 independent strategies.
You have a handful of correlated prompt variants.

In live trading, correlated agents fail together.

6) Agent evolution is overfitting to recent noise

The evolution system promotes and mutates agents based on recent paper performance.

In crypto, short-term edge is regime-sensitive and unstable. So this creates:

in-sample optimization
regime overfitting
lucky winner promotion
unstable descendants with weak statistical backing

Instead of discovering robust alpha, it is likely amplifying recent noise.

7) The production behavior contains randomness in important paths

I found random behavior in live decision paths, including:

degraded agents randomly skipping cycles
agent coin count varying randomly
synthetic price seeding jitter
fallback predicted move randomness
auto-deploy allocation jitter

That makes the system less reproducible and harder to debug.

For a trading app, randomness in production should be very tightly controlled and isolated.

8) The evaluation framework is still too disconnected from real expectancy

The system tracks:

accuracy
score
confidence
streaks
calibration
validation summaries

But the app still gives too much importance to non-economic measures.

Problems:

“correct” is defined by threshold crossing, not whether the move was worth trading
neutral outcomes can soften bad predictions
score changes are not tightly tied to net dollar expectancy
prediction quality and trade quality are partially separate systems

So agents can optimize toward:

nice-looking accuracy
nice-looking score
not necessarily good net P&L
9) 5m trading is especially weak under your own cost model

Using your current fee/slippage assumptions, 5m trades are very hard to justify.

With your default 5m stop/target geometry, the breakeven win rate is much higher than the dashboard accuracy numbers make it look.

So 5m calls can:

resolve as “correct”
still be poor trades
generate churn
feed misleading reinforcement back into the system
10) The system still relies on LLMs for the hardest part

LLMs can help with:

context synthesis
qualitative sentiment
structured narrative summaries
combining inputs

But they are not ideal as the primary numerical forecasting engine for short-horizon price moves.

Your app asks them to do:

micro directional prediction
confidence estimation
stop/take-profit estimation
regime-aware action selection

That is the most fragile possible use of LLMs in trading.

They should be downstream helpers, not the core signal generator.

11) Trade management is more sophisticated than the prediction engine that feeds it

You have:

position sizing
portfolio risk caps
drawdown halts
daily loss limits
EV gates
trailing extensions
fleet correlation brake

That part is not terrible.

But the incoming signal is still weak, noisy, and badly scored.

So the system is basically:
good plumbing around a bad forecast target

Risk management can reduce damage, but it cannot create edge.

Why it can’t make good predictions and trades

The failure chain looks like this:

Indicators and context are partly noisy or synthetic
LLMs make directional calls on a weak numerical foundation
“Stable” / abstain is under-supported
Prediction success is judged on too-small moves
Confidence calibration is reversed

That is why the app can feel advanced and still fail in actual trading quality.

The fixes that matter most

In order:

1) Fix calibration immediately
reverse the bias labeling
reverse the calibration adjustment sign
add unit tests for calibration buckets and bias behavior
2) Stop rewarding non-tradable moves
raise prediction success thresholds so they exceed friction meaningfully
or resolve predictions by net expectancy, not raw direction
3) Add true abstain support
allow "stable" or "no_trade" end-to-end
make abstention a first-class valid output
measure quality of abstention separately
4) Disable evolution until the base strategy is statistically proven
freeze agents
stop mutating them from recent paper performance
5) Remove synthetic intraday history from anything used for edge estimation
do not build live signal features from fabricated price paths
use real historical bars only
6) Stop using LLMs as primary forecasters

Better architecture:

numeric model for direction/return distribution
LLM only for context/sentiment/regime commentary
meta-layer combines actual quantitative forecasts with risk rules
